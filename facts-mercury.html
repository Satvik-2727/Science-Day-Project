    MERCURY
    ATMOSPHERE
    Mercury has an exosphere, an extremely thin and wispy atmosphere. It's so thin that it's often considered nearly absent compared to the dense atmospheres of other planets.
    GEOLOGY
    One of the most prominent features on Mercury is the Caloris Basin, a huge impact crater about 960 miles (1,550 kilometers) in diameter. It's surrounded by concentric rings and was formed by a massive impact early in Mercury's history.
    COMPOSITION
    Mercury's composition is predominantly rocky and metallic. It has a large metallic core, accounting for about 60% of its mass. This core is believed to be primarily composed of iron and is thought to be molten, generating a magnetic field.
    JUPITER
    ATMOSPHERE
    Lightning occurs on Jupiter, producing bright flashes that have been observed in its clouds. The planet also exhibits auroras near its poles, caused by charged particles interacting with its magnetic field.
    GEOLOGY
    Jupiter boasts an extremely powerful magnetic field, the strongest in the solar system, generated by the metallic hydrogen layer surrounding its core. This magnetic field extends far beyond the planet and interacts with its moons and surrounding space.
    COMPOSITION
    Jupiter's composition closely resembles that of a failed star. In fact, it's sometimes referred to as a "failed star" because if it had accumulated more mass during its formation, it might have become a star rather than a gas giant planet.
    SATURN
    ATMOSPHERE
    Near Saturn's north pole, Saturn features a remarkable hexagonal-shaped jet stream discovered by the Cassini spacecraft. This persistent hexagon, larger than Earth's diameter, is believed to be formed by powerful winds in the upper atmosphere.
    GEOLOGY
    Saturn's most prominent feature is its intricate ring system composed of countless particles ranging in size from tiny grains to boulders. These rings are not solid structures but rather collections of orbiting ice, rock, and dust particles, possibly remnants of comets, asteroids, or shattered moons.
    COMPOSITION
    Saturn generates its magnetic field from its liquid metallic hydrogen layer deep within the planet. This magnetic field extends far beyond the planet and interacts with its rings and surrounding space.
    URANUS
    ATMOSPHERE
    Uranus rotates on its side, with an axial tilt of about 98 degrees. This extreme tilt causes its poles to receive more prolonged periods of sunlight or darkness, leading to unique seasonal variations and atmospheric effects.
    GEOLOGY
    Uranus has a system of faint rings composed mainly of dust, rock, and ice particles. These rings, though not as prominent as Saturn's, contribute to the planet's geophysical interactions.
    COMPOSITION
    Uranus is classified as an ice giant rather than a gas giant like Jupiter or Saturn due to its higher proportion of water, ammonia, and methane ices compared to the more hydrogen-rich gas giants.
    NEPTUNE
    ATMOSPHERE
    Similar to Jupiter's Great Red Spot, Neptune once had a massive storm known as the Great Dark Spot. However, this storm observed by Voyager 2 in 1989 seemed to have vanished in subsequent observations by the Hubble Space Telescope, showcasing the dynamic nature of Neptune's atmosphere.
    GEOLOGY
    Beneath its thick atmosphere, Neptune likely has a small, rocky core surrounded by layers of ice, water, methane, and ammonia. The pressure and temperature within Neptune increase towards its core, creating different states of matter.
    COMPOSITION
    It's theorized that Neptune has a small, rocky core surrounded by layers of icy materials, including water, methane, and ammonia. Its atmosphere consists of hydrogen, helium, and traces of methane.
